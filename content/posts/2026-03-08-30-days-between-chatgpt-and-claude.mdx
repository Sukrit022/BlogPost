In February I forced myself into an annoying experiment: rotate primary models every day and keep notes.

No formal benchmark harness. Just real work.

Tasks included:

- reading and summarizing papers,
- drafting internship notes,
- brainstorming startup ideas,
- revising short writing.

I expected one model to clearly dominate.

That did not happen.

The biggest differences were in interaction behavior under pressure, especially when prompts were messy or I changed requirements halfway.

## How I tracked it

Each session got a quick score on:

1. first-answer usefulness,
2. correction quality,
3. constraint retention,
4. time to acceptable output.

This kept me honest. Otherwise I would just remember flashy moments.

## Surprise #1: response posture matters a lot

Some models were better brainstorming partners.
Some were better execution partners.

When I gave vague prompts, certain models expanded possibility space in useful ways.
When I gave strict constraints, other models followed structure better and wasted less time.

I now think less in terms of "best model" and more in terms of "best posture for this stage of work."

That framing reduced frustration immediately.

## Surprise #2: correction loops feel different

This one is underrated.

When I flagged one error, some models fixed exactly that error.
Others rewrote entire sections and introduced fresh mistakes.

I did not realize how much this affects trust until I tracked it.

In practice, precise patching can outperform higher raw intelligence for day-to-day productivity.

If every correction restarts the draft, you lose momentum.

## Surprise #3: context fatigue appears in subtle ways

Long chats are still messy.

After enough turns, I saw gradual slippage:

- dropped formatting constraints,
- forgotten exclusions,
- drift toward generic responses.

Not dramatic memory loss. More like slow context erosion.

A simple fix helped a lot: reset threads earlier and restate hard constraints as a short checklist.

That one habit improved output quality more than most prompt tricks.

## My practical setup now

I use a very unglamorous stack:

- one model for idea expansion,
- one model for structured drafting,
- one quick verification pass for important outputs.

I initially thought this was overkill.

Now it feels normal because it reduces avoidable errors and keeps me from over-trusting one system.

Also, the "single best model" narrative feels less useful in week-to-week practice. Mixed workflows outperform purity.

## A small thing that changed quality a lot

I started ending prompts with one line:

"Before final answer, list anything you are uncertain about in one short bullet list."

This cut overconfident mistakes more than I expected.

It also made review faster because uncertain points were explicit.

## What I would tell someone starting now

Do not spend week one picking a forever model.

Spend week one designing a repeatable workflow:

- clear task split,
- correction loop discipline,
- lightweight verification.

Model choice still matters. Workflow design matters more than people think.

## Closing thought

The longer I use these systems, the more I think model choice is less like picking a smartphone and more like choosing teammates with different working styles.

Compatibility with your workflow beats abstract leaderboard rank more often than people expect.

I wish I had learned that earlier. Would have saved me a lot of random tool-hopping.
