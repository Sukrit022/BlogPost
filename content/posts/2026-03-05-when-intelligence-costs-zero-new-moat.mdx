I was looking at API pricing changes over the last two years and had one uncomfortable thought:

If intelligence keeps getting cheaper this fast, a lot of AI business models are standing on thin ice.

The cost curve has moved so quickly that old assumptions about margin and differentiation are already outdated.

## Cheap intelligence changes what is valuable

When a core capability becomes abundant, value shifts away from raw access.

We have seen this in other tech cycles:

- compute became cheaper,
- storage became cheaper,
- bandwidth became cheaper,
- and business value moved to integration, distribution, and workflow lock-in.

AI is heading the same way.

If high-quality generation is available for fractions of a cent per request, "we call a strong model API" is not a moat.

It is table stakes.

## Why thin wrappers get squeezed

This part sounds harsh, but I think it is true.

Thin wrappers usually have this profile:

- low switching cost,
- minimal proprietary data,
- no deep workflow integration,
- feature set easy for platforms to absorb.

When base model providers improve, these products lose differentiation quickly.

I have seen this pattern repeat enough times now that I treat it as default risk.

## Where defensibility is moving

If I were evaluating an AI startup today, I would look for three assets first:

1. Proprietary data loops
Does product use generate unique signal that improves outcomes over time?

2. Workflow depth
Is the product embedded into daily operations with real switching cost?

3. Distribution advantage
Does the team control a channel others cannot easily copy?

Then I would look at trust layers:

- auditability,
- reliability,
- compliance fit,
- human override controls.

In regulated sectors, trust infrastructure can be more defensible than model choice.

## A simple founder test

Ask one hard question:

If model quality doubles and API price halves next year, does my product become stronger or weaker?

If weaker, the current moat is probably not real.

If stronger, you are likely building leverage on top of commoditization rather than fighting it.

## VC perspective: what gets funded in this phase

From recent patterns, investor conviction seems stronger when startups show:

- compounding feedback data from real usage,
- proof of integration into expensive workflows,
- measurable reliability advantage,
- clear path to distribution beyond paid ads.

Decks centered on "we picked the best model" feel less persuasive now.

Model choice still matters, but it is no longer the whole story.

## Jevons paradox and why cheaper AI may increase total spend

A common assumption is: if model calls get cheaper, total AI spend should fall.

History suggests the opposite can happen.

Jevons paradox says when a resource becomes cheaper and more efficient, usage often expands enough that total consumption rises.

We saw this with compute, cloud, storage, and bandwidth.

AI may follow the same curve:

- per-call cost drops,
- new use cases become viable,
- call volume explodes,
- total spend remains large or grows.

So "intelligence gets cheap" does not mean AI economics become irrelevant.

It means competition moves to who can deploy cheap intelligence into high-frequency workflows with strong monetization.

## The barbell effect in value capture

If this trend continues, value can barbell to two ends:

- bottom of stack: chips, power, infra,
- top of stack: companies with strong distribution and proprietary interaction data.

Middle-layer undifferentiated wrappers get squeezed.

That is why I think the fight is no longer "model vs app" in simple terms.

It is "commodity capability vs proprietary context."

## Bear case: incumbents absorb everything

There is a harsh bear case for startups.

If platform incumbents bundle strong AI into existing products fast enough, many standalone AI point solutions may struggle to retain users unless they own unique workflow depth.

In this scenario, startup defensibility must come from:

- deeply verticalized execution,
- distribution channels incumbents cannot cheaply replicate,
- proprietary data loops tied to real operations.

Otherwise, feature parity pressure arrives quickly.

## Open question

If model quality converges and pricing keeps collapsing, will startup winners come mostly from new distribution channels and proprietary data surfaces, or can pure product experience still create durable moat without unique data ownership?

## A monthly moat dashboard I would use

For teams operating in this environment, I think a monthly "moat dashboard" is useful:

1. percentage of product value driven by proprietary data,
2. percentage of retention driven by workflow lock-in,
3. margin sensitivity to model-provider price changes,
4. time-to-copy estimate for top features,
5. share of usage from owned vs rented distribution channels.

If those numbers trend in the wrong direction, you are probably building a beautiful commodity.

Better to detect that early than after your category gets bundled by incumbents.

## Strategic positioning models for this phase

In this environment, I see three viable positioning models for startups:

Model A: distribution-first

- own user relationship,
- integrate commodity intelligence underneath,
- defend with channel and brand trust.

Model B: workflow-first

- solve painful, frequent tasks,
- embed deeply in daily operations,
- defend with switching costs and reliability.

Model C: data-first

- capture unique interaction data,
- improve outcomes continuously,
- defend with feedback-loop compounding.

Most weak products accidentally pick Model D: model-access-first.

That one usually decays fastest.

## Downside risk for founders

If you mistake temporary API arbitrage for durable moat, you may optimize growth on top of a disappearing advantage.

That can produce impressive short-term metrics and ugly long-term retention once incumbents catch up.

So growth quality matters as much as growth speed in this cycle.

## What I would ask in every board update

One board-level prompt can keep this honest:

"If our model provider launched our core feature tomorrow at zero price, what part of our value still survives?"

If the answer is fuzzy, the strategy needs revision now, not after the market forces it.

## Closing thought

Intelligence getting cheaper is not bad news. It is sorting pressure.

The winners will not be the teams with the fanciest wrapper.

They will be the teams that turn cheap intelligence into hard-to-replace outcomes.

That is the moat now.
