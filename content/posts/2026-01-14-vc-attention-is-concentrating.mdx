If you only read headlines, it looks like every AI startup is getting funded.

When I spent winter break digging through deals, public partner posts, and the AI Index summaries, I got a different picture: strong AI funding overall, but concentration into fewer startup profiles.

I initially thought this was temporary market caution. Now I think it is a structural response to one reality: base model capability is improving quickly and becoming easier to access.

If everyone has access to strong models, investors look harder at what cannot be copied quickly.

## Where capital still moves quickly

Across deals I tracked, faster conviction showed up when startups had at least one of these:

- A hard-to-copy distribution channel.
- A real data flywheel from customer usage.
- Deep workflow integration into systems of record.
- Strong trust/reliability infrastructure in regulated or high-cost environments.

In simple terms, investors are asking one brutal question:

"If base models get much better next year, does this startup become stronger or replaceable?"

That question filters a lot of decks very quickly.

## What I think got misread in 2025

A common narrative was "AI apps are hot." True but incomplete.

What looked like broad enthusiasm was often selective enthusiasm:

- broad interest,
- narrow conviction,
- concentrated check-writing.

Large rounds clustered around teams that proved compounding advantage, not just attractive demos.

## Why generic wrappers are getting squeezed

This sounds harsh, but I think it is mostly true: if your product advantage is prompt craft and UI polish alone, your half-life is short.

As model providers ship stronger defaults, thin wrapper features are absorbed quickly. The bar moved from "cool demo" to "durable wedge" in less than a year.

That doesn't mean app-layer AI is dead. It means the app layer needs real leverage beyond API stitching.

## The leverage test I now use

When I evaluate startup ideas, I ask:

1. What improves each week from customer usage?
2. What part of that improvement is hard for competitors to buy?
3. If the frontier model API changed tomorrow, would the product still hold value?

Strong answers include:

- proprietary correction data,
- deep workflow context,
- trust, audit, and verification layers,
- customer-specific integration depth.

Weak answers include:

- "we will always pick the best model"
- "our prompt engineering is better"

Those are not stable moats for long.

## A better lens: compounding assets

I've started evaluating AI startup ideas using one question:

What improves in your system each week that competitors cannot buy immediately?
What improves in your system each week that competitors cannot buy immediately?

That is still the best filter I have found.

## Small disagreement with common VC commentary

A lot of public commentary frames AI investing as model race vs app race. I think that dichotomy is too simple.

The real game looks more like control points across the stack:

- data capture,
- workflow surface,
- trust and compliance,
- customer distribution,
- operational reliability.

Model quality matters, but it is one layer.

## Closing thought

If I were pitching today, I would spend less time on benchmark screenshots and more time showing evidence of compounding advantage from live usage.

That is what seems to move a startup from "interesting" to "fundable" in this cycle.

The market is not rejecting AI apps. It is rejecting shallow AI apps.
