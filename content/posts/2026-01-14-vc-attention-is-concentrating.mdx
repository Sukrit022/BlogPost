From outside, it looks like VCs are funding everything with "AI" in the deck.

From what I tracked over winter break, the pattern feels more selective: capital is concentrating into categories with clearer defensibility signals, while generic application-layer bets get filtered harder than people admit.

I initially thought this was just late-2025 risk aversion. Now I think it's a structural response to model commoditization.

## Where capital still moves quickly

The fastest conviction seems to appear when a startup has one of these:

- proprietary distribution channel with hard-to-copy workflow lock-in,
- unique data feedback loop that compounds,
- deep integration into critical enterprise systems,
- strong eval/reliability layer in regulated or high-cost domains.

In plain words, investors are asking: "If base models improve 10x, do you get stronger or erased?"

## Why generic wrappers are getting squeezed

This sounds harsh, but I think it is mostly true: if your product advantage is prompt craft and UI polish alone, your half-life is short.

As model providers ship better default features, many wrapper-level features get absorbed upstream. The bar moves from "cool demo" to "durable wedge."

That doesn't mean app-layer AI is dead. It means the app layer needs real leverage beyond API stitching.

## A better lens: compounding assets

I've started evaluating AI startup ideas using one question:

What improves in your system each week that competitors cannot buy immediately?

Good answers include:

- proprietary human correction data,
- workflow embeddings tied to operational systems,
- trust and audit infrastructure,
- domain-specific eval harnesses.

Weak answers include "we'll switch to the best model" because everyone can do that.

## Small disagreement with common VC commentary

A lot of public commentary frames AI investing as model race vs app race. I think that dichotomy is too simple.

The real game feels like "control points in the value chain": data, distribution, workflow surface, trust, and verification.

Model quality matters, but it is one layer.

## Closing thought

If I were pitching an AI startup right now, I would spend less time on benchmark slides and more time proving compounding learning loops from real usage.

Because that is what seems to turn "interesting" into "investable" in this cycle.
