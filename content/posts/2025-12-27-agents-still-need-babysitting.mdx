I went into this month with two extreme opinions fighting in my head:

- "Agents are overhyped wrappers around tool calls."
- "Agents will replace huge chunks of white-collar workflows soon."

After actually building and testing a few mini agent loops, I landed in the boring middle.

Agents are useful. But unattended reliability is still far from where the hype suggests.

## Where agents genuinely helped me

I built one agent for research triage: collect sources, summarize differences, produce a short position note.

When scoped tightly, it saved me real time. Especially on repetitive subtasks like:

- extracting key claims from 5-6 articles,
- normalizing them into one format,
- drafting a first-pass comparison.

I don't want to undersell that. It was enough value to keep using it.

## Where things broke in annoying ways

The failure pattern wasn't dramatic errors. It was brittle execution.

Examples from my logs:

- Tool call order drifted after one unexpected response shape.
- The agent retried the wrong step instead of recovering context.
- It confidently completed a workflow while silently skipping one required check.

This is the part most demo videos ignore. A workflow can look 90% done and still be unusable because the missing 10% is the compliance or validation step.

## Why this happens (my best guess)

I think agents stack uncertainty across layers:

1. model reasoning uncertainty,
2. tool interface uncertainty,
3. environment uncertainty,
4. objective ambiguity.

Any one layer can be manageable. Together, they amplify small mistakes.

I initially thought better model intelligence alone would solve this. Now I think orchestration design and guardrails matter just as much.

## What made my agent less flaky

The biggest improvements came from boring engineering:

- strict typed tool schemas,
- step-by-step state logs,
- explicit "done criteria" per stage,
- cheap verifier pass before final output.

Not glamorous, but reliability jumped.

Also, I stopped pretending "fully autonomous" was the right goal. Human-in-the-loop checkpoints on high-impact steps made the system feel way more practical.

## Closing thought

My contrarian take now is simple: the winning agent products in the near term may look less autonomous than expected.

They will probably be collaborative systems with sharp boundaries, not magical co-workers running free.

And honestly, that's fine. Useful + predictable beats autonomous + fragile.
