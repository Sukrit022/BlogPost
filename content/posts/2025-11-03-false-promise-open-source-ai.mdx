The phrase "open source AI" has started to feel like elastic marketing language.

Stretch it enough and it means almost anything.

A lot of companies now say "open" when what they really offer is model weights and maybe a limited license.

Useful? Yes.

Open source in the traditional sense? Not always.

## Open weights is not the same as open source

This is the distinction I wish more people said clearly.

Open weights usually means you can run the trained model and inspect behavior.

True open source, historically, means you can inspect, modify, rebuild, and redistribute the full system under terms that preserve those freedoms.

In AI, that full system includes more than weights:

- data pipeline choices,
- filtering decisions,
- training code,
- training recipe,
- evaluation pipeline.

If those are missing, you are interacting with a black box that is merely easier to access.

That can still be valuable. But it is different.

## Why this matters beyond semantics

At first I thought this was mostly a purity argument from open-source people.

Then I saw why founders and researchers should care.

If you cannot reproduce a model end-to-end, you cannot fully audit where behavior comes from.

You also cannot reliably answer tough questions like:

- what data biases were introduced,
- what legal constraints are embedded,
- what part of behavior changed after fine-tuning,
- what can be improved without vendor dependency.

So yes, you can build products with open-weight models.

But you still inherit important uncertainty.

## The "free sample" problem

Here is the blunt test I use now:

If a company disappears tomorrow, can the community recreate this model pipeline from provided artifacts?

If the answer is no, it is probably not open source. It is a free sample with a good distribution package.

That is not an insult. It is just category clarity.

And category clarity matters when founders design long-term dependency risk.

## OSI stepping in was inevitable

The Open Source Initiative trying to define Open Source AI more explicitly was not random policy theater.

It was a response to market confusion.

Without shared definitions, every launch post can claim openness while hiding key components that make reproducibility possible.

Then users assume rights they do not actually have.

That gap creates legal and technical surprises later.

## Why big tech incentives push "open washing"

This part is uncomfortable but predictable.

"Open" messaging gives companies credibility with developers.

At the same time, withholding full training assets protects competitive edge and reduces risk.

So the market found a middle state:

- release enough to be adopted,
- keep enough private to preserve control,
- market it using language associated with traditional open source.

Can you blame them? From a business standpoint, maybe not.

But we should still call the model what it is.

## Practical takeaway for builders

When evaluating a supposedly open model, do a five-minute checklist:

1. Are weights available under usable terms?
2. Is training code available?
3. Is data provenance documented enough to assess risk?
4. Is the recipe reproducible in practice, not just in theory?
5. Can you legally ship a commercial product with minimal ambiguity?

If most answers are unclear, treat it as "open-access model," not true open source.

That framing will save you from legal and platform surprises.

## Closing thought

I am not anti open-weights. They have accelerated experimentation massively.

I just think we should stop pretending every open-weight release is open source.

If you cannot build the box, inspect the process, and replicate the result, you are not looking at open source. You are looking at a very useful black box with generous API access.
