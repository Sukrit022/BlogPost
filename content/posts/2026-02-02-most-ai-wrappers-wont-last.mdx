"It is just a wrapper" became the easiest way to sound smart in AI conversations.

I used that phrase too, and I was often wrong.

The phrase hides the real question:

Can users switch away from your product tomorrow without pain?

If yes, you are fragile.
If no, you may have real defensibility even if you started as a wrapper.

This is one of the biggest opinion changes I had over the last six months.

## Replaceable wrappers have a pattern

The weak pattern usually looks like this:

- Single-shot text generation.
- No memory of user workflow context.
- No improvement loop from user corrections.
- No integration into high-friction systems.

If that's your product, model upgrades from a large provider can wipe out your differentiation in one release cycle.

I have seen this happen fast. A team launches a nice AI helper. Two months later, model providers add similar capability natively. User value collapses.

## Durable AI apps look different

The stronger pattern is less flashy:

- Deeply embedded in a painful workflow.
- Learns from user edits and corrections.
- Handles exceptions and edge cases, not only happy-path prompts.
- Lives in channels users already open daily.

At that point, the model becomes a component, not the product.

This is exactly how infrastructure value gets created in other software categories too. AI just makes the pace faster.

## Two examples that clarified this for me

Example A: generic meeting summary app

- input: transcript,
- output: summary,
- little context retention,
- easy to replace.

Example B: vertical compliance copilot for a specific operations team

- integrated into internal systems,
- knows approval flow,
- tracks policy versions,
- saves audit history,
- escalates edge cases.

Both "wrap" a model. Only one is likely to be sticky.

## The hidden moat: operational context

The moat I underestimated most was operational context.

If your system knows how a specific team works, what constraints matter, which approvals are required, and where errors are expensive, you are no longer a thin wrapper. You are workflow intelligence.

That context accumulates through usage, not through model size.

And this matters because context quality compounds. Every correction, exception, and policy change makes the product harder to copy.

## Practical checklist I use now

When evaluating an AI startup idea, I ask:

1. If the underlying model got dramatically better next quarter, would this product become stronger or irrelevant?
2. Does the product get better from real customer behavior data?
3. Is there a painful workflow step solved end-to-end, including edge cases?
4. Could a competitor copy this in 30 days without customer access?

If the honest answer to #4 is yes, trouble.

I also ask one extra question now:

What breaks for the customer if they uninstall this tomorrow?

If nothing breaks, pricing power will eventually break.

## Closing thought

So yes, most generic wrappers probably won't survive. But AI apps as a category are not the problem.

The problem is shallow product strategy pretending model access is a moat.

The winners may look boring in a demo and indispensable after six months of real use.

That is usually a good sign.
